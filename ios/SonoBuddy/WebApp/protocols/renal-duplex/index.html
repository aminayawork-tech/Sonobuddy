Renal Artery Duplex Ultrasound

advanced 45–75 min· Curvilinear 2–5 MHz (aorta/renal origins); Linear 5–9 MHz (intrarenal)

Patient Position

Supine for aorta and right renal; Left lateral decubitus for left renal artery. Fasting 4–6 hours mandatory.

Indications

Resistant hypertension, suspected renovascular hypertension, flash pulmonary edema, renal failure on ACE inhibitor/ARB, renal transplant surveillance, FMD evaluation

1

Aorta Survey — Grayscale + Doppler

Begin with the aorta at the level of the renal arteries (approximately L1–L2). Measure AP aortic diameter. Sample aortic PSV with angle correction ≤60°. This will be used to calculate the Renal-Aortic Ratio (RAR = renal PSV ÷ aortic PSV). Normal aortic PSV: 80–120 cm/s.

Curvilinear 2–5 MHz

Tips

→

Patient must be fasting — bowel gas is the single biggest obstacle on this exam

→

Aortic PSV is required for RAR calculation — obtain BEFORE renal arteries

→

Probe orientation: slightly left of midline, angled toward right with patient in slight Trendelenburg if needed for better penetration

2

Renal Artery Origins — Right

With aorta in sagittal view, angulate right to locate the right renal artery origin. It arises from the right lateral aortic wall, just below the SMA origin, and passes posterior to the IVC. Use the IVC as an acoustic window. Sample at origin, mid, and distal right renal artery with angle ≤60°.

Curvilinear 2–5 MHz

Tips

→

Right renal artery passes BEHIND the IVC — use IVC as a window (posterior angulation)

→

Color Doppler helps locate the vessel before placing spectral gate

→

Aliasing/color mosaic = elevated velocity = stenosis at that segment

→

Normal renal artery PSV: <180 cm/s; RAR <3.5

3

Renal Artery Origins — Left

The left renal artery arises from the left lateral aorta at the same level as the right. With the patient turned to right lateral decubitus (or scanning from flank), identify the left renal artery from origin to hilum. Angle correction is essential. Sample at origin, mid, and distal. Left renal artery is typically harder to visualize due to overlying bowel.

Tips

→

Left lateral decubitus position opens the retroperitoneal window for the left renal artery

→

Alternative: posterolateral approach through the left flank with patient in right lateral decubitus

→

If not visualized: document attempted approach and use indirect (intrarenal) tardus-parvus as surrogate

4

Intrarenal Doppler — Right Kidney

Switch to linear probe or high-frequency curvilinear. Scan right kidney in coronal plane. Sample interlobar or arcuate arteries at upper, mid, and lower poles. Calculate Resistive Index (RI) = (PSV − EDV)/PSV at each location. Average of 3 measurements. Normal RI: 0.58–0.70.

Curvilinear 3–5 MHz or Linear 5–7 MHz

Tips

→

Tardus-parvus waveform (delayed systolic upstroke + blunted peak): indirect sign of proximal stenosis — measure acceleration time (AT) — normal <70 ms

→

Acceleration Index (AI): normal >3 m/s²; <3 m/s² suggests proximal stenosis

→

Elevated RI (>0.80): obstruction, parenchymal disease, rejection (transplant)

→

Asymmetric RI (>0.05 between kidneys): possible hemodynamically significant stenosis on low-RI side

5

Intrarenal Doppler — Left Kidney

Repeat intrarenal Doppler sampling for the left kidney in the same manner as the right. Sample upper, mid, and lower pole interlobar arteries. Document waveform morphology. Compare RI bilaterally — asymmetry is clinically significant.

Tips

→

Left flank approach through spleen or posterolateral intercostal window

→

Normal: gentle rounded systolic peak with continuous forward diastolic flow

→

Absent diastolic flow + elevated RI: severe parenchymal disease or acute obstruction

6

Kidney Size & Parenchyma

Measure each kidney in long axis (craniocaudal length). Normal: 9–12 cm. Assess cortical thickness (normal ≥1.5 cm) and echogenicity relative to adjacent liver (right) and spleen (left). Increased cortical echogenicity = medical renal disease. Asymmetry >2 cm: clinically significant — consider renal artery stenosis on the smaller side.

Tips

→

Small kidney (<9 cm) on the side of suspected stenosis strongly supports renovascular etiology

→

Cortical thinning (<1 cm) = significant parenchymal loss from chronic ischemia or medical disease

→

Post-void bladder assessment: urinary retention can cause bilateral hydronephrosis — look at collecting systems

→

Assess for incidental renal masses or cysts (Bosniak classification)
