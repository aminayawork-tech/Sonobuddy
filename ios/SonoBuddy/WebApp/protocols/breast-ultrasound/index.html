Breast Ultrasound

intermediate 20–40 min· High-frequency linear 10–15 MHz

Patient Position

Supine with ipsilateral arm raised above head; slight contralateral oblique to flatten breast against chest wall. Expose one breast at a time.

Indications

Palpable lump, mammographic finding (mass, asymmetry, distortion), nipple discharge, axillary adenopathy, pain/inflammation, screening adjunct in dense breasts, guided biopsy/aspiration

1

Patient Positioning & Setup

Position patient supine with ipsilateral arm raised overhead. For large breasts, use slight oblique (pillow under ipsilateral hip). Apply generous warm gel. Use the highest frequency that provides adequate penetration (typically 12–15 MHz for superficial structures, 10 MHz for deep/large breasts).

Linear 12–15 MHz

Tips

→

Arm elevation flattens the breast and reduces tissue thickness

→

Start on the symptomatic side or the side with the abnormality

→

For dense or large breasts, drop frequency to 10–12 MHz for deeper structures

→

Always scan both breasts and axillae for complete assessment

2

Systematic Survey — Radial Scan

Scan each breast systematically using radial and anti-radial planes (following the ductal anatomy), sweeping from nipple to periphery in a clock-face pattern. Cover all 4 quadrants: UOQ, UIQ, LOQ, LIQ, and the retroareolar (subareolar) region. Document the nipple and areola separately.

Linear 12–15 MHz

Tips

→

Radial/anti-radial planes follow ductal architecture — more sensitive for intraductal lesions than transverse/longitudinal

→

Do not skip the retroareolar region — this is where most ducts converge

→

Use clock-face notation: "10 o'clock, 3 cm from nipple" (nipple = center of clock)

→

Tail of Spence (axillary tail) must be scanned — it contains breast tissue and lymph nodes

3

Axillary Lymph Node Survey

Scan the ipsilateral axilla. Normal nodes are oval with a thin hypoechoic cortex (≤3 mm) and echogenic fatty hilum. Document any enlarged, rounded, or cortically thickened nodes. Measure the largest suspicious node in two dimensions.

Linear 10–15 MHz

Tips

→

Normal lymph node: kidney-bean shape, echogenic hilum, cortex ≤3 mm

→

Suspicious: cortex >3 mm, loss of fatty hilum, round shape, eccentric cortical thickening

→

Level I nodes (lateral to pectoralis minor) are the primary drainage nodes for most of the breast

→

If axillary nodes are abnormal, document distance from skin surface for potential biopsy planning

4

Characterize Any Mass — Morphology

For each identified mass, document using ACR BI-RADS lexicon: Shape (oval/round/irregular), Orientation (parallel/not-parallel), Margin (circumscribed/not circumscribed — indistinct/angular/microlobulated/spiculated), Echo pattern (anechoic/hyperechoic/complex cystic and solid/hypoechoic/isoechoic/heterogeneous), Posterior features (no change/enhancement/shadowing/combined).

Tips

→

Orientation: parallel (wider than tall) = more likely benign; not-parallel (taller than wide/vertical) = suspicious

→

Circumscribed margins = more likely benign; ANY non-circumscribed feature = suspicious

→

Simple cyst: anechoic, thin imperceptible walls, posterior enhancement, no internal vascularity — BI-RADS 2

→

Complicated cyst: homogeneous low-level echoes, no solid component — BI-RADS 3 if no prior malignancy history

5

Characterize Any Mass — Measurements

Measure each mass in three orthogonal dimensions: longest axis (length), perpendicular to longest (width), and depth (AP). Record measurements in two planes (radial and anti-radial or longitudinal and transverse). Always document orientation relative to the nipple using clock position and distance from nipple (e.g., "9 o'clock, 4 cm from nipple").

Tips

→

Three measurements are required for volume estimation and follow-up comparison

→

Measure outer edge to outer edge including any echogenic halo

→

For irregular masses, measure the maximum dimension in each plane

→

Always freeze and caliper — do not estimate on real-time

6

Vascularity — Color / Power Doppler

Apply color or power Doppler to evaluate vascularity. Document: avascular, internal vascularity, peripheral vascularity, or both. Hypervascular masses with penetrating vessels are more suspicious than peripheral-only flow. Simple cysts should be avascular.

Tips

→

Reduce PRF (scale) for low-flow detection in small masses

→

Power Doppler is more sensitive than color Doppler for slow flow

→

Vascularity alone does not determine malignancy — integrate with morphology

→

Fibroadenomas can be hypervascular — always correlate with shape/margin features

7

Elastography (if available)

If strain or shear-wave elastography is available, apply to suspicious masses. Malignant lesions tend to be stiffer than surrounding tissue. Document qualitative color map (strain elastography) or quantitative kPa values (shear wave). Stiff lesion = blue on most color maps = higher suspicion.

Tips

→

Elastography is an adjunct — do not use alone to upgrade or downgrade BI-RADS

→

Apply gentle, consistent pressure for strain elastography — too much pressure gives false readings

→

Shear-wave threshold for suspicion: >80 kPa on most platforms

→

Cysts appear soft on strain elastography despite being anechoic (fluid transmission artifact)

8

Skin, Nipple & Chest Wall

Evaluate the skin (normal ≤2 mm; thickening >2 mm, especially if unilateral = suspicious), nipple/areolar complex (subareolar mass, dilated ducts, nipple retraction), and chest wall (pectoralis major, ribs, pleura). Note any Cooper's ligament thickening or skin tethering overlying a mass.

Tips

→

Skin thickening + underlying mass + lymphedema = breast edema — consider inflammatory carcinoma

→

Dilated duct(s) in the retroareolar region: follow the duct — intraductal papilloma can be subtle

→

Always document if a mass contacts or invades the pectoralis — changes surgical planning

→

Measure skin thickness at the site of any suspected skin involvement

9

Assign BI-RADS Category

Assign a final ACR BI-RADS US assessment category: 1 (Negative), 2 (Benign), 3 (Probably benign — <2% malignancy, 6-month follow-up), 4A/4B/4C (Suspicious — tissue sampling recommended), 5 (Highly suggestive of malignancy), 6 (Known biopsy-proven malignancy). BI-RADS 0 = incomplete, needs additional imaging.

Tips

→

BI-RADS 3 requires 6-month ipsilateral follow-up, then 12-month bilateral, then 24-month — total 2 years

→

BI-RADS 4A: low suspicion (≥2–<10%); 4B: moderate (≥10–<50%); 4C: high (≥50–<95%)

→

Never assign BI-RADS 3 to a patient with a new palpable lump — go directly to 4 and biopsy

→

If mammogram already assigned BI-RADS, do not downgrade on ultrasound alone without radiologist review
